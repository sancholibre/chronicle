# Immigration Fears Through History

## Summary

Each wave of immigration to the US (and other receiving countries) triggered remarkably similar fears: they won't assimilate, they'll take jobs, they bring crime/disease, they'll change our culture. These fears were expressed about Irish, Germans, Italians, Jews, Chinese, Mexicans, and Muslims—often using identical language. The feared groups consistently assimilated within 1-2 generations.

## What Happened

### Irish Immigration (1840s-1860s)
- Potato famine drove mass emigration
- "No Irish Need Apply" signs in businesses
- Feared as Catholic threat to Protestant nation
- Accused of drunkenness, violence, political corruption
- Know-Nothing Party (1850s) formed specifically to oppose Irish/Catholic immigration
- Outcome: Irish became quintessentially "American" within two generations

### Chinese Immigration (1850s-1882)
- Railroad construction and Gold Rush drew workers
- "Yellow Peril" fears
- Accused of stealing jobs, moral depravity, inability to assimilate
- Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) - first law excluding ethnic group
- Outcome: Restrictions lasted until 1943; Chinese Americans now highest-income ethnic group

### Southern/Eastern European Immigration (1880s-1920s)
- Italians, Poles, Jews, Greeks arrived in millions
- "New immigrants" contrasted unfavorably with "old" (Northern European)
- IQ tests (biased toward English speakers) "proved" inferiority
- Accused of crime, radicalism, disease
- Immigration Act of 1924 established national quotas
- Outcome: Their grandchildren are now considered "white" and fully assimilated

### Mexican Immigration (1940s-present)
- Bracero Program (1942-1964) brought agricultural workers
- "Illegal alien" framing emerged
- Accused of taking jobs, not learning English, crime
- Cycles of enforcement and amnesty
- Outcome: Second-generation achievement gaps largely close; Spanish use declines

### Muslim/Middle Eastern Immigration (2001-present)
- Post-9/11 suspicion
- "Muslim ban" executive order (2017)
- Accused of terrorism risk, incompatible values
- Debates about Sharia law, assimilation
- Outcome: Still unfolding; research shows similar assimilation patterns

## Preconditions

For immigration fears to emerge:
1. **Visible difference** - Language, religion, appearance, customs
2. **Economic anxiety** - Competition for jobs or resources
3. **Rapid numbers** - Fast growth makes change visible
4. **Cultural distance** - The further from "native" culture, the more fear
5. **Triggering events** - Crime, terrorism, economic downturns blamed on immigrants
6. **Political entrepreneurship** - Leaders who benefit from stoking fear

## Contemporary Perspective

**What nativists said (remarkably consistent across eras):**
- "They don't assimilate like previous groups did"
- "They take jobs from real Americans"
- "They bring crime and disease"
- "Their religion/culture is incompatible with democracy"
- "We're not against immigration, just ILLEGAL immigration"
- "Our ancestors came LEGALLY" (often inaccurate)
- "They're changing our country"

**Why these arguments recurred:**
- Each generation forgets previous groups were once "other"
- Visible first-generation differences mask second-generation assimilation
- Confirmation bias notices immigrant crime, ignores native crime
- Economic fears are easy to project onto visible scapegoats

## Actual Outcomes

**What the nativists got wrong:**
- Every group they said couldn't assimilate, did
- Crime rates are consistently lower for immigrants than natives
- Economic effects are generally positive (immigrants fill niches)
- Language shift to English happens within two generations
- "Incompatible" religions became mainstream (Catholicism, Judaism)

**What the nativists got right:**
- Immigration DOES change culture (just not in the apocalyptic way feared)
- Transition periods DO involve friction
- Some immigration waves DID coincide with labor market disruption
- Rate of immigration matters—faster change is harder to absorb

**What both sides missed:**
- Assimilation is bidirectional—America changes too
- Economic impacts are complicated (gains to some, losses to others)
- Integration takes time—judging first generation is misleading
- Policy design matters more than total numbers

## Hindsight

**With the benefit of history, we now understand:**

1. **The "last acceptable immigrant" is always in the past** - Every nativist movement praised previous immigrants while fearing current ones. Benjamin Franklin worried about Germans; later generations worried about Irish while praising Germans.

2. **First-generation struggles are poor predictors** - Language retention, ethnic enclaves, poverty—all fade dramatically by second/third generation.

3. **"Assimilated" is a moving target** - What counts as American changes. Pizza and tacos are now American food.

4. **Economic fears are usually overstated** - Immigrants often complement rather than substitute for native workers.

5. **The children tell the story** - Second-generation outcomes are strikingly similar across origin countries.

## Generalizable Insights

1. **What feels unprecedented usually isn't** - The specific group is different; the pattern is the same.

2. **Give it a generation** - Most integration concerns resolve themselves with time.

3. **Watch the second generation** - They're the real test, not their parents.

4. **Economic anxiety finds scapegoats** - If not immigrants, it would be something else.

5. **Culture is more absorbent than feared** - Nations adapt; they don't shatter.

## Key Differences To Watch

When might current immigration be different?

1. **Scale and speed** - Global mobility is greater than ever
2. **Welfare states** - Economic calculation is different with social programs
3. **Multiculturalism vs. assimilation** - Ideological shift may slow integration
4. **Diaspora connectivity** - Technology allows maintaining home ties in new ways
5. **Declining native fertility** - Immigration is a larger share of population growth

But: these "this time is different" arguments were made about every previous wave too.
